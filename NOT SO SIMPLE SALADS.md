### **Introduction**

Let me be honest with you right out of the gate: I have opinions about salads. Strong ones.

If you serve me a salad with baby carrots cut into little rounds, I will smile. I will say thank you. And I will silently, internally, *judge you*. Not forever. But briefly. Firmly.

Most of the salads in this book are meals. Hearty, fully-committed meals — the kind that don't apologize for being substantial. But I also know that sometimes you need something lighter. A summer evening. A day where lunch already got out of hand. A giant pot roast dinner that still somehow left you wanting something bright and alive on the side. You still deserve a flavor bomb in those moments — it just doesn't need to be dinner. So I've organized the book accordingly: the big, bold meal salads up front, and a lighter collection in the back for when you need the flavor without the commitment.

What this book is *not* is a collection of quick, easy salads. There is nothing wrong with quick, easy salads — they have their place, and that place is not here. This book is for people who want a *salad experience*. The kind where you stop mid-bite and think: what is happening right now, and why is this so good. Layered. Textured. Built with intention.

I've been making salads for as long as I can remember. I grew up in the kitchen — it's basically my natural habitat — and somewhere along the way, salads became *my thing*. Anyone who's eaten at my table knows this. I take them seriously. I think about the acid. I have strong feelings about texture. I will julienne a vegetable by hand because it's meditative and also because I want the result to be exactly right.

The salads here are ones I've built, lovingly borrowed, adapted, and obsessed over for years. Some were inspired by chefs I deeply admire — Ottolenghi, obviously, who has permanently rewired how I think about flavor combinations. Some came out of the most unexpectedly great food I've ever eaten at Google's Mixit café, where the in-house chefs are quietly doing things with salads that have no business being as good as they are. I've been sitting there eating something extraordinary, mentally reverse-engineering it, then going home to make my version. This book is full of those moments.

A note on the dressings: some of them have been made so many times they're locked in. Others are still alive — still evolving, still being tweaked. You'll see a lab marker \[🧪\] next to those. Think of it as an invitation. Try the recipe, make it your own, and tell me what you changed. The best recipes are always a conversation.

This is a labor of love. Make a mess. Eat well.

— Jaclyn

### **How to Use This Book**

This book is divided into two sections.

The first — and longer — section is **Meal Salads**. These are the main event. They are hearty, layered, and fully intended to be dinner. Bring them to a potluck and watch people be surprised that a salad is the thing everyone's talking about. Make them on a Tuesday because you deserve a Tuesday that tastes like that.

The second section is **Lighter Salads**. Still packed with flavor — nothing in this book is timid — but scaled back in heft. These are for summer evenings, or the days when lunch already got out of control, or when you need something bright alongside something heavy. They are not afterthoughts. They are just not trying to be dinner.

**A note on the lab marker \[🧪\]** Some dressings are marked with a beaker symbol. That means the dressing is still evolving — I've made it enough times to know it's delicious, but I haven't fully locked in the ratios. The version in the book is my best current starting point. Try it, taste it, adjust it, and if you land on something better: I genuinely want to know.

**A note on technique**

*Julienning* — I do this with a sharp knife. You can use a mandoline. I won't judge you. Much.

*Salt* — I use kosher salt unless I specify Maldon flaked salt. When I say Maldon, I mean it — it's a finishing salt and it behaves differently. I'll always tell you which one.

*Pepper* — I use both freshly ground black pepper and powdered black pepper, and occasionally white pepper powder. I specify which in each recipe. They are not interchangeable.

*Fresh everything* — fresh lemon, fresh lime, fresh ginger, fresh garlic. Always. If you're in a pinch, pre-prepared works. But fresh is the difference between good and the kind of salad people ask you about.

### **1\. Cauli-fornia Dreamin'**

*Serves 2–3 · 30 mins*

**Headnote** This was the salad that started it all — the one I fell for at Google and immediately needed to reverse-engineer. All the textures, that citrus dressing, but the crispy capers are what put it completely over the top. They're the hardest part to replicate at home and absolutely, completely worth every second of effort. Don't you dare skip them.

**Salad Ingredients**

* Roasted cauliflower  
* Castelvetrano olives  
* Parmesan cheese, shaved  
* Fennel, shaved  
* Arugula  
* Quinoa  
* Crispy capers  
* Grilled chicken

**Dressing: Citrus Vinaigrette**

* ¾ cup orange juice  
* 3 Tbsp olive oil  
* 3 Tbsp lemon juice (1 lemon)  
* 1½ Tbsp lime juice (1 lime)  
* 1 tsp honey  
* 1 tsp red wine vinegar  
* ½ tsp kosher salt  
* ½ tsp chili pepper flakes  
* Pinch of ground white pepper  
* 1 garlic clove, grated

* 1 tsp Korean chili flakes

### **3\. Banh Mi Over**

*Serves 2 · 35 mins (plus marinating time)*

**Headnote** Everything I love about a banh mi, deconstructed into a bowl. The pickled vegetables are essential — and honestly, surprisingly easy to make at home. Totally worth it. Don't shortcut them. The marinade does the heavy lifting on the chicken, so give it time.

**Salad Ingredients**

* Radish  
* Pickled carrot, julienned  
* Pickled cucumber  
* Cilantro  
* Chicken (marinated in fish sauce, lime juice, garlic, jalapeño)  
* Green onion  
* Romaine lettuce

**Dressing: Soy Miso** 🧪

* 2 tsp coconut sugar  
* 1 tsp white miso  
* ½ lime, juiced  
* 1 Tbsp rice wine vinegar  
* 3 Tbsp grapeseed oil  
* 1 tsp sesame oil

### **4\. Keep Cobb and Carry On**

*Serves 2–3 · 25 mins*

**Headnote** The Cobb is my OG hearty salad. I love it. Always. This version pairs it with a vinaigrette instead of the usual ranch or blue cheese dressing — that's non-negotiable for me. And the sautéed onions? Those came from a tiny restaurant in the small town that sits at the gateway to Yosemite. I've never looked back.

**Method Note** Sauté the white onion low and slow — at least 15 minutes, until almost jammy. This is the step most people skip. Don't.

**Salad Ingredients**

* Mixed greens  
* Tomatoes, diced  
* Cucumber  
* Hard boiled egg, sliced  
* Thick-cut bacon, chopped  
* Grilled chicken  
* Avocado  
* Blue cheese  
* Sautéed white onion

**Dressing: Greek Balsamic** 🧪

* 3 Tbsp balsamic vinegar  
* ½ cup grapeseed oil  
* ½ tsp onion powder  
* ½ tsp garlic powder  
* 1 tsp dried oregano  
* ½ tsp dried mustard  
* ½ tsp salt  
* ¼ tsp black pepper  
* 1 tsp maple syrup

### **5\. Thai'd and True**

*Serves 2 · 30 mins (plus marinating time)*

**Headnote** This one has a very special place in my heart. I made it for a friend during a road trip across the US — we'd just stopped by their place for a night, my husband and I had climbed our first-ever 14er that day, and then we came back and made this salad. * Spinach  
* Red onion  
* Cherry tomatoes  
* Fresh cilantro  
* Fresh mint  
* Flank steak

**Dressing: Nam Jim**

* 2 Tbsp fish sauce  
* 1½ limes, juiced  
* 1 Tbsp grapeseed oil  
* 2½ tsp white sugar  
* 1 thai chili  
* Cilantro stems — half a bunch  
* Pinch of salt  
* White pepper  
* 1 garlic clove

**Flank Steak Marinade**

* Soy sauce  
* Garlic  
* Ginger  
* Red chili paste

*Marinate at least 1 hour, overnight is better. Slice thin against the grain.*

### **6\. Niçoise To Meet You, Where You Bean?**

*Serves 2 · 35 mins*

**Headnote** This salad feels like summer lunches in Silicon Valley — elevated yet approachable, hearty and filling and somehow still light. The caper berries are the real note here. The sesame-chili crust on the tuna is my addition to the classic, and a Niçoise is one of the great composed salads of the world. I will die on that hill.

**Salad Ingredients**

* Caper berries  
* Cherry tomatoes  
* Boiled potatoes  
* Blanched green beans  
* Cucumber  
* Red onion  
* Hard boiled egg  
* Mixed greens  
* Seared ahi tuna

**Dressing: Lemon Dijon**

* 1 lemon, juiced  
* 4 Tbsp extra virgin olive oil  
* 1 garlic clove, grated  
* 1 tsp Dijon mustard  
* ¼ tsp honey  
* Pinch of salt  
* Pinch of black pepper

**Ahi Tuna Rub**

* Sesame seeds  
* Black sesame seeds  
* Korean chili flakes  
* Flaked salt

*Press the rub firmly onto the tuna. Sear in a very hot pan — 60–90 seconds per side. The center should stay raw.*

### **8\. Romaine Calm: Caesar's Got a Flame**

*Serves 2 · 30 mins*

**Headnote** I've never loved Caesar salad. I know. Crazy. And yet — when you grill romaine, something genuinely magical happens. It transforms. If you thought a caterpillar turning into a butterfly was something, you should try this. It's a completely different salad and I am now obsessed.

**Salad Ingredients**

* Grilled romaine hearts  
* Bacon  
* Homemade croutons  
* Parmesan cheese

**Dressing: Caesar** 🧪

* 2 egg yolks  
* 4 anchovy fillets, minced to a paste  
* 1 garlic clove, grated  
* 2 Tbsp fresh lemon juice  
* 1 tsp Dijon mustard  
* 1 tsp Worcestershire sauce  
* ½ cup olive oil  
* ¼ cup grapeseed oil  
* ¼ cup finely grated Parmesan  
* Cracked black pepper to taste

**Grilled Romaine Hearts**

* Cut romaine hearts in half lengthwise  
* Drizzle with olive oil, season with salt and pepper  
* Grill cut-side down on high heat, 2–3 minutes until charred  
* Chop into bite-sized pieces

### **13\. Bacon Me Crazy**

*Serves 1–2 · 15 mins*

**Headnote** Love me that runny fried egg. The whole premise here is that the yolk breaks and becomes part of the dressing — it's the reason the dressing itself is so minimal. Trust the process. The egg is not optional.

**Salad Ingredients**

* Spinach  
* Iceberg lettuce  
* Red onion  
* Cucumber  
* Avocado  
* Cherry tomatoes  
* Millionaire's bacon

**Dressing: Simple** 🧪

* 2 Tbsp rice vinegar  
* 4 Tbsp EVO oil  
* ½ tsp Maldon salt flakes  
* ¼ tsp white pepper

**Finishing Touch** Top with a runny fried egg. The yolk breaks and mixes into the dressing — this is the whole point.

### **14\. Salad Days and Sunny Ways**

*Serves 1–2 · 20 mins*

**Headnote** Blanched asparagus doesn't get enough love and I'm here to change that. Crudo is lively, but something about blanched asparagus in a salad is just so good — all the flavor, bright as anything. Pair it with prosciutto and a runny egg and you have a perfect plate.

**Salad Ingredients**

* Prosciutto  
* Frisee lettuce  
* Cucumber  
* Red onion  
* Radish  
* Tomatoes  
* Blanched asparagus

**Dressing: Simple** 🧪

* 2 Tbsp champagne vinegar  
* 4 Tbsp grapeseed oil  
* ½ tsp Momofuku Tingly Salt  
* ¼ tsp black pepper  
* 1 tsp maple syrup

**Finishing Touch** Top with a runny fried egg. Same logic as Bacon Me Crazy — the yolk is part of the dressing.

### **15\. The Caprese Edit**

*Serves 2 · 10 mins*

**Headnote** Three ingredients. No hiding. Wait for peak tomato season — a magnificent heirloom in August will make this one of the best things you eat all summer. A mediocre tomato in February will make you wonder what the fuss is about. Patience is the recipe.

* Fresh basil

**Dressing: No Measure**
